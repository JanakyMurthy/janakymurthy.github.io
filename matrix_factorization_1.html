Hence it is not really necessary to compute the prime factorization of yyy and xxx. But there are problems where we really need to be able to factorize large composite numbers. Say you are working for an agency that asks you to monitor the conversation between two parties AAA and BBB. The two parties have agreed upon a large secret prime number yyy which you are unaware of. Suppose AAA wants to communicate some message xxx (which again turns out to be a large prime number). AAA is not naive enough to send xxx directly to BBB, else you would have intercepted the message easily. Instead, AAA sends the product (y⋅x)(y \cdot x)(y⋅x). Since BBB has yyy, it can easily recover xxx from this product. On the other hand, you can retrieve this conversation only if you are able to factorize (y⋅x)(y\cdot x)(y⋅x).

Consider a number x=p1n1⋅p2n2⋅…⋅pknkx = p_1^{n_1} \cdot p_2^{n_2} \cdot \ldots \cdot p_k^{n_k}x=p1n1​​⋅p2n2​​⋅…⋅pknk​​ where pip_ipi​'s are prime numbers. Let us write the prime factorization PxP_xPx​ of xxx as follows:

Px={(p1,n1),(p2,n2),…,(pk,xk)}P_x = \{(p_1,n_1), (p_2,n_2), \ldots,(p_k,x_k) \}Px​={(p1​,n1​),(p2​,n2​),…,(pk​,xk​)}

For the prime factorization problem, we could ask the following questions:

  1. Existence: Can every (positive) integer be written as a product of prime factors?
  2. Uniqueness: Given any two prime factorizations PxP_xPx​ and QxQ_xQx​ of a (positive) integer xxx, is it the case that Px=QxP_x = Q_xPx​=Qx​?
  3. Algorithm: Do we know how to compute the prime factorization of a given integer? If yes, how efficient it is?

The answer to the first two questions is yes due to the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic. To avoid digression from the main topic, I will not go into the third question. But the point is, similar questions can be asked for any factorization problem.
